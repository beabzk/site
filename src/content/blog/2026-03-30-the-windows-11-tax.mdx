I am typing this on an operating system I actively dislike. 

Windows 11 is a perfect example of enshittification. Microsoft took a reliable tool and degraded the user experience to push its own corporate agendas. Instead of focusing on making a faster or more stable operating system, they decided to cram AI into every corner. I don't need Copilot in Notepad, and I definitely don't need a feature taking constant screenshots of my desktop under the guise of an AI memory aid. I want an OS that manages my hardware efficiently and stays out of my way.

Beyond the forced AI integration, the basic user interface feels heavy. Windows Explorer is the most used application on the system, yet it performs terribly. Third-party file managers do a much better job, which is embarrassing for a trillion-dollar company. Then there are the constant updates. You can rarely shut down your machine without waiting for an unskippable update that seemingly changes nothing except resetting your default browser preferences.

When you complain about Windows, the immediate response from a lot of people is to buy a Mac. But any Apple product is a hard no for me. 

It doesn't matter how fast their chips are or how good the build quality is. You pay a premium for the device, but you don't really own it. Apple does. You are locked into their walled garden, and you have to do things the "Apple way" or you simply can't do them. 

There is also a strange mindset around their products. In many places around the world, an iPhone or MacBook is treated as a status symbol. It is automatically assumed to be the superior device, even when there are better phones and computers in the exact same price range. As someone who likes to build tools and understand how things work under the hood, I refuse to buy into an ecosystem that is openly hostile to hardware repairability.

My ideal machine would be a Framework laptop running a Linux distribution. That setup combines physically modular, repairable hardware with an open and customizable operating system. I manage Linux servers and I have used different distros on my desktop for a few days at a time, so I have the software background to appreciate it. 

So why don't I switch? 

My mechanical engineering degree makes it impossible. My coursework relies heavily on professional software like SolidWorks and AutoCAD. These programs do not run natively on Linux. Trying to run heavy 3D parametric modeling software through a virtual machine causes a massive performance hit that makes doing actual work miserable. 

It is the same story with the games I play. Valorant uses a kernel-level anti-cheat system that explicitly blocks Linux and virtual machines. 

My choices are clear. I can give up my primary engineering tools and my favorite games, or I can stay on Windows 11. 

For now, I am stuck doing the latter. I spend my time disabling ads and debloating the start menu just to get a clean workspace. I write software to automate my routines and fix daily annoyances, but unfortunately, you can't write a script to fix an operating system's broken business model.
